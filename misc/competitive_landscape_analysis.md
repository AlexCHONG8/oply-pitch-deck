# Oply Competitive Landscape Analysis
## Global MDF & Wood-Based Panel Manufacturers
**Date: May 2026 | Prepared for Investor Due Diligence**

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The global MDF market reached approximately **121.87 million cubic meters** in 2025, valued at roughly **$9.49 billion in Asia-Pacific alone**. China dominates production with **~40.64 million m3/year** of fiberboard capacity. Oply's competitive set spans Malaysian incumbents with Sarawak operations, Chinese export-heavy manufacturers, and global panel giants pursuing sustainability transitions. No major competitor currently produces agri-waste/biomass MDF at commercial scale in Southeast Asia, creating a potential first-mover window.

### Agricultural Waste Board Operations in China

China has the world's most developed agricultural residue panel industry:
- **Wheat straw, rice straw, and bagasse** are used as feedstock for MDF and particleboard
- Key producers include companies in Shandong and Jiangsu provinces
- Government policy has at times incentivized agricultural waste utilization to reduce crop burning
- Products are marketed as "eco-boards" or "straw boards"
- Quality perception remains lower than wood-based MDF, limiting premium applications

### Export Dynamics to SE Asia

- China exports significant MDF volumes to Malaysia, Indonesia, Philippines, and Vietnam
- Chinese MDF is typically 15-30% cheaper than domestic SE Asian production
- Price advantage driven by scale, government subsidies, and lower labor costs
- Growing trade friction: several SE Asian nations have investigated or imposed anti-dumping measures
- Thailand is the largest MDF exporter within ASEAN (72% of SE Asian regional exports)

**Competitive Threat to Oply:** HIGH. China's scale advantage and aggressive export pricing pose the biggest competitive challenge. However, quality concerns, trade barriers, and sustainability perceptions create differentiation opportunities for Oply's agri-waste MDF.

## 8. MARKET & REGULATORY ENVIRONMENT

### Malaysia MDF Import Tariffs

| HS Code | Description | MFN Import Duty | ASEAN (ATIGA) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4411.11 | MDF, thickness <= 5mm | 0-5% | 0% |
| 4411.19 | MDF, thickness > 5mm <= 9mm | 0-5% | 0% |
| 4411.21 | MDF, thickness > 9mm | 0-5% | 0% |

**Key Notes:**
- ASEAN-origin MDF enjoys 0% duty under ATIGA -- Thailand, Indonesia, Vietnam all benefit
- Sales tax of 10% may apply on imports
- China does not get ASEAN preferential rates, but has its own FTA with Malaysia (ACFTA) that may reduce duties
- Official tariff schedule available from Royal Malaysian Customs Department (JKDM)

### Anti-Dumping on Chinese MDF

- Malaysia's Ministry of Investment, Trade and Industry (MITI) is the authority for anti-dumping investigations
- Malaysia has historically imposed anti-dumping duties on various Chinese products
- **No specific, currently active anti-dumping duty on Chinese MDF was confirmed** in publicly available 2024-2025 records
- However, the threat of anti-dumping action is real -- several ASEAN nations (Indonesia, Philippines, Thailand) have investigated Chinese panel dumping
- **Status: MONITOR.** This is a potential policy tool that could benefit domestic MDF producers including Oply

### Malaysian Government Policy: Timber vs. Biomass-Based Products

**Policy Direction: STRONGLY FAVORABLE for biomass/agri-waste panels**

| Policy Area | Details |
|---|---|
| **Sarawak Biomass Hub** | Sarawak actively attracting foreign investment (Singapore, China, Japan) in biomass industries |
| **Downstream Empowerment** | Sarawak government incentivizing downstream timber processing, biomass production, bamboo industries |
| **Bamboo Masterplan** | STIDC's Sarawak Bamboo Industry Development Masterplan (2019); target of 10,000 ha bamboo plantations |
| **National Biomass Action Plan** | 2023-2030 framework includes Sarawak biomass data for bioeconomy development |
| **Forest Policy Reform** | Since 2019, Sarawak forestry policy significantly reformed with stronger environmental protection |
| **Automation Incentives** | Government offering automation incentives for downstream timber/biomass processing |
| **Certification Push** | MTCS/PEFC certification expanding; Reduced Impact Logging guidelines |
| **Carbon Trading** | STIDC-UiTM study (2025) on bamboo-based carbon credit potential |

**Key Signal:** The Sarawak government has mandated that timber companies convert portions of their concessions into bamboo plantations. This directly creates a non-wood fiber feedstock that could be used for MDF production. STIDC describes bamboo as a "game changer" for northern Sarawak.

## 10. KEY STRATEGIC INSIGHTS FOR OPLY

### Competitive White Space
1. **No major player produces agri-waste/biomass MDF in Southeast Asia.** This is the core opportunity.
2. **Chinese agri-waste board exists** but is perceived as lower quality and faces trade barriers.
3. **Sonae Arauco's BioReFiber X** is the closest comparable innovation globally, but it uses recycled wood fiber (not agri-waste) and is not available in SE Asia.
4. **Sarawak's policy environment is uniquely favorable** -- bamboo plantations, biomass incentives, downstream processing support, and timber concession reform all align with Oply's model.

### Competitive Moats to Build
1. **Feedstock cost advantage:** Agri-waste/bamboo fiber sourced locally in Sarawak at lower cost than imported timber
2. **Sustainability premium:** First-mover on certified agri-waste MDF in SE Asia (carbon credits, ESG credentials)
3. **Policy alignment:** Sarawak government actively seeking biomass industry investment -- potential for grants, tax incentives, concession access
4. **Quality differentiation:** Target JIS/EN standard certification to command premium over Chinese budget boards

### Key Risks
1. **Samling and Rimbunan Hijau** have deep Sarawak political connections and could respond aggressively (price cuts, political pressure) to protect market share
2. **Chinese MDF export pricing** (15-30% below domestic) can undercut any SE Asian producer on price
3. **Anti-dumping policy** could help or hurt -- depends on whether Oply's product is classified as MDF (HS 4411) or a novel product
4. **Feedstock availability** for agri-waste is seasonal and supply chains are less developed than timber
